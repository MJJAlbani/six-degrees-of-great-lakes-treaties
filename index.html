Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties

Visualization

Introduction

During the late-eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, treaty negotiations served as critical sites of contact between Anishinaabeg – members of the Three Fires Confederacy of the Ojibwe, Odawa, and Potawatomi – Americans, and peoples of mixed ancestry across the Great Lakes. While the United States government attempted to exert total dominion over all the territory it claimed on paper, Indigenous inhabitants of the Great Lakes region strived to remain in their homelands. Over the course of several decades, many Anishinaabe and American individuals emerged as diplomats at multiple treaty negotiations. "Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties" is a digital cultural heritage project that seeks to illustrate the connections between such individuals who presented themselves as representatives for their communities and nations time and time again. Although the Great Lakes region may have been geographically vast, recurring diplomatic entanglements rendered it more socially close-knit than one might expect.

Sources

"Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties" arose from a desire to see the names of individuals who signed onto late-eighteenth and nineteenth-century Indigenous land cession treaties affecting what would become the state of Michigan dynamically displayed. Thus, it began by drawing heavily upon Native American Treaties resources from Central Michigan University. As the map on the left suggests, CMU identifies 11 treaties that led to cessions of future Michigan lands.
