Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties

Visualization

Introduction

During the late-eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, treaty negotiations served as critical sites of contact between Anishinaabeg – members of the Three Fires Confederacy of the Ojibwe, Odawa, and Potawatomi – Americans, and peoples of mixed ancestry across the Great Lakes. While the United States government attempted to exert total dominion over all the territory it claimed on paper, Indigenous inhabitants of the Great Lakes region strived to remain in their homelands. Over the course of several decades, many Anishinaabe and American individuals emerged as diplomats at multiple treaty negotiations. "Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties" is a digital cultural heritage project that seeks to illustrate the connections between such individuals who presented themselves as representatives for their communities and nations time and time again. Although the Great Lakes region may have been geographically vast, recurring diplomatic entanglements rendered it more socially close-knit than one might expect.

Sources

"Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties" arose from a desire to see the names of individuals who signed onto the late-eighteenth and nineteenth-century Indigenous land cession treaties that shaped what would become the state of Michigan dynamically displayed. Thus, it began by drawing heavily upon Native American Treaties resources from Central Michigan University. As the map to the left suggests, CMU identifies 11 treaties that resulted in cessions of future Michigan lands. Approaching this digital cultural heritage project considering only those documents, however, would neglect to fully factor in how Anishinaabeg who called what is now Michigan home (indeed, whose descendents continue to call it home) transgressed the national and territorial borders Euro-American authorities were attempting to cement. Hunting, fishing, producing maple sugar, and interacting with their homelands in a multitude of other ways propelled Ojibwe, Odawa, and Potawatomi peoples to tred beyond the lines Euro-American powers claimed were drawn upon their lands and waters. "Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties," then, utilizes a broader corpus that interested researchers are free to view, copy, and comment upon.

In many ways, "Six Degrees of Great Lakes Treaties" could be viewed as a more geographically focused companion to the "US Treaty Signer Project". Both aim to assist in the navigation of dense government documents that ofen obscure the identities of Indigenous actors.
